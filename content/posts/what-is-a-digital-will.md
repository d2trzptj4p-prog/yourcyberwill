# What Is a Digital Will? Why Traditional Wills Fail in the Tech Age

For centuries, estate planning was simple. You sat down with an attorney, wrote out your asset distributions on a piece of paper, signed it before witnesses, and filed it away. When you passed away, probate court executed those instructions.

But we now live in an era dominated by advanced encryption, cloud data, decentralized finances, and strict multi-factor authentication. 

If your estate plan relies solely on a traditional paper will, your family is headed for a technical crisis. Here is what a **digital will** is, and why traditional estate planning fails to protect your technical footprint.

---

## Why Traditional Wills Fail the Tech Test

A traditional paper will has two structural flaws that make it fundamentally incompatible with modern digital accounts:

### 1. The Publicity Problem
Once a traditional will enters the probate process, it becomes a matter of public legal record. Anyone can look it up. This means you **cannot** write your phone PINs, master password phrases, or 24-word crypto seed phrases directly into a standard legal will. Doing so would expose your sensitive credentials to the entire world.

### 2. The Enforcement Gap
A judge can sign an order demanding a bank transfer physical funds, but a judge cannot sign an order that changes the laws of mathematics. If your family inherits a locked smartphone or an encrypted hard drive without the decryption password, no amount of legal documentation will open it. Apple, Google, and decentralized blockchains cannot bypass their own security architectures to extract your data.

---

## Enter the Digital Will: An Automated Hand-Off

A digital will is not just a digital copy of a paper document. It is a **technical protocol** designed to securely store and automatically hand over access credentials, encrypted notes, digital assets, and files to your beneficiaries if you go silent.

Instead of writing keys on paper, a digital will utilizes a cryptographic mechanism known as a *dead man's switch*.
